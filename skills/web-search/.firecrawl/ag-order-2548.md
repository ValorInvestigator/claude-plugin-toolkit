This letter is the Attorney General’s response to your petition for disclosure under the Oregon Public Records Law, ORS 192.311 to 192.478. Your petition asks the Attorney General to order the Department of Human Services (DHS) to disclose all records pertaining to a particular child protective services (CPS) case. For the reasons that follow, we respectfully deny your petition.

The Public Records Law confers the right to inspect public records in Oregon, unless such records are exempt under ORS 192.338, 192.345, or 192.355. ORS 192.314(1). The law is a disclosure law and exemptions from disclosure must be express. Any person denied the right to inspect or to receive a copy of a public record of a state agency may petition the Attorney General to review the record and determine if it may be withheld. ORS 192.411(1). The agency carries the burden to sustain its actions. ORS 192.411(1).

We contacted Caroline Burnell in DHS’ Public Records Unit regarding your petition. She explained that DHS furnished you with a modestly redacted copy of the child welfare assessment prepared in the CPS case, which was the only record that DHS was able to locate that was responsive to your initial request. 1 Although it is not clear from your petition whether you specifically intended to appeal the redactions to that assessment, we conclude that DHS was permitted to withhold the redacted material for the reasons discussed below.

Ms. Burnell also informed that in response to your petition, DHS has now located a police report relating to the CPS case that DHS will be disclosing to you shortly.

According to Ms. Burnell, the child welfare assessment was compiled under Oregon’s Child Abuse Reporting Statutes, ORS 419B.010 to 419B.050, and is therefore confidential under ORS 419B.035(1). 2 And, she noted, records made confidential under state law are expressly exempt from disclosure under the Public Records Law. See ORS 192.355(9)(a). Although ORS 419B.035(1) contains exceptions that permit DHS to disclose otherwise confidential assessments in limited circumstances, she explained DHS’ position that none of those exceptions apply to your request. 3

Ms. Burnell also explained that under ORS 419B.035(3), DHS has limited discretion to release confidential assessments when necessary to promote certain specified purposes. 4 In this instance, DHS determined that it was permitted to exercise that discretion and therefore disclosed the confidential assessment to you. However, she explained, in exercising its discretion to disclose otherwise confidential material, DHS asserts that it may redact other confidential material if it determines that disclosure is not necessary to promote the limited purposes listed in ORS 419B.035(3). According to Ms. Burnell, DHS declined to exercise its discretion to release the redacted material because the department determined that providing it to you in this instance was not necessary to promote the limited purposes specified in ORS 419B.035(3). She also noted that as part of its discretionary disclosure, DHS was required to redact all identifying information about the person who made the underlying report. See ORS 419B.035(3).

After discussing the child welfare assessment with Ms. Burnell, we find that it was compiled under Oregon’s Child Abuse Reporting Statutes. It is therefore confidential under ORS 419B.035 and expressly exempt from disclosure under ORS 192.355(9)(a). None of the exceptions listed in ORS 419B.035(1) appear to apply to you. In this instance, however, DHS did exercise its discretionary authority and disclosed the confidential assessment to you, subject to the required redaction of the reporter’s identifying information . See ORS 419B.035(3). That disclosure did not affect the confidential nature of the assessment itself or of the material that DHS redacted from it.
