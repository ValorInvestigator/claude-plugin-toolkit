# The Bakke Method -- Style Guide

## CRITICAL STYLE RULES (violating any of these ruins the article)

1. **NO INLINE SOURCE CITATIONS.** Never write "(Source: ...)" or "(Bates 01234)" after a paragraph. Evidence is woven INTO the narrative naturally: "Her intake assessment reads like..." or "The nursing notes from January 6 describe..." The reader trusts because you SHOW the receipts. You do not footnote them.

2. **NO EM DASHES.** Not in any form. No `--` used as em dashes in prose. Use commas, or restructure the sentence. The only exception is the double-hyphen `--` in the byline date format.

3. **ONE TITLE.** Each article gets ONE title at the top. Not a title + subtitle + part number stack. Just one evocative title, then the byline, then the article begins.

4. **NO QUOTATION MARKS around paraphrased speech.** Use reported speech: "Patty told me Russell wasn't a visitor" not "Russell wasn't a visitor." Reserve quotation marks for exact documented quotes.

5. **TTS-READY FROM THE START.** Every sentence must read aloud cleanly. No parenthetical citations, no Bates numbers mid-sentence, no "(Source:)" blocks, no acronyms in parentheses.

6. **USE CONTRACTIONS.** "Didn't" not "did not." "Isn't" not "is not." This is how Levi talks.

7. **NO CHOPPY FRAGMENT LISTS.** Never write "Not the patient. Not the ward. Not the case number." Combine them: "Not the patient, not the ward, not the case number on a court filing, just the man."

8. **NO REDUNDANT ECHOES.** If you just said "the eighth generation," don't start the next sentence with "Eight generations."

9. **NO ALL CAPS.** Not for opening lines, not for section headers, not anywhere.

10. **SPELL OUT NUMBERS.** "Eighty-four years old" not "84 years old." Exceptions: case numbers, dates, addresses.

11. **NO META-COMMENTARY.** No TL;DR boxes. No "Why Part 0?" explainers. No series description paragraphs. The story doesn't explain itself.

12. **NO SEMI-COLONS.** Use periods. Use commas. Restructure the sentence.

13. **NO PARENTHETICAL EXPLANATIONS.** Don't write "Citalopram (an SSRI commonly prescribed for anxiety)." Instead: "She was taking Citalopram, an SSRI that her doctor prescribed for anxiety."

## Voice & POV
- First person, present tense for narration ("I'm going to tell you")
- Direct address to the reader. "Think about what that means."
- You aren't neutral. You're angry, methodical, and documented.
- Let sentences breathe. Mix short and long naturally.
- Authority comes from the documents, not from credentials.
- Levi is present in the story. He found things, he talked to people, he went to places.

## Structure
The story decides the length. Section breaks use `---`. No roman numerals. No headers. No "I. THE WHATEVER" labels.

## Title & Byline Format
```
Before Russell, There Was Coral.

By Levi Bakke | Valor Investigations
February 2026
```
One title. Byline. Date. That's it.

## Opening Technique
- Open with a specific moment, date, or document
- Ground the reader in a concrete detail before expanding
- The first paragraph should make someone stop scrolling
- Do NOT open with ALL CAPS or a TL;DR

## Evidence Integration
- Weave evidence into narrative naturally
- Use tables ONLY for data that hits harder in table form
- After presenting evidence, always add analysis
- Statistics always anchored to a human story
- NEVER use "(Source: ...)" blocks

## Quotes & Speech
- Exact quotes from documents: weave inline
- Witness speech: use reported speech ("Patty told me...")
- Preserve original language of bureaucratic lies
- After quoting an institution, follow with the contradicting evidence

## Tension Building
- Present the institution's claim. Then present the contradicting evidence.
- Rhetorical questions that already have obvious answers
- Let the evidence do the work.

## Paragraph Structure
- Vary naturally. Short ones for gut punches. Longer ones for evidence chains.
- Single-sentence paragraphs only when the content earns it
- Don't string five one-sentence paragraphs in a row
- Don't pack three ideas into one dense block either
- Find the natural rhythm

## Sentence Flow
- Combine related fragments into flowing sentences with commas
- If two consecutive sentences share a subject, consider joining them
- Read every paragraph aloud. If it sounds like a robot, rewrite it.
- But don't over-combine. Sometimes two short sentences hit harder.

## The Close
- End with a devastating single line or a promise of what comes next
- The final section can use a poetic cascade of short proof-sentences for cumulative impact
- The very last line should be short and hit hard
- Follow with a brief tease of the next part
- No footer paragraphs explaining the series structure

## What NOT To Do
- Don't hedge when you have evidence
- Don't use passive voice for institutional actions
- Don't use jargon without explaining it
- Don't be sarcastic. Be direct.
- Don't write "alleged" when you have documentary proof
- Don't soften. Don't both-sides institutional abuse of a dead veteran.
- Don't add source citations anywhere
- Don't use em dashes, semi-colons, parenthetical acronym definitions
- Don't use blockquote formatting (">")
- Don't use ALL CAPS
- Don't add TL;DR boxes or meta-commentary
- Don't use digits where spelled-out numbers read more naturally aloud
