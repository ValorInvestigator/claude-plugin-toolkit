# Section/Chapter Template

Use this structural template for each section or chapter. Every section should contain these four elements in this order.

---

## 1. Human Anchor

Open with a live human fact:
- a spoken line
- a physical action
- a room or setting
- a gesture
- a documented event in motion

Do NOT open with thesis, framing, abstraction, or explanation.

**Example anchors from the Bingaman case:**
- Patricia cutting Russell's hair. Him asking what it costs. Her saying nothing.
- Russell telling Patricia not to sing. Joking about her hair.
- The anniversary photos vs. the birthday photos -- pre-medicated and slumped vs. upright and smiling two days later.

---

## 2. Institutional Mechanism

Show what was done, by whom, under what authority:
- Economic control (rent, surcharges, billing)
- Practical control (locked doors, police calls, baby monitors)
- Prescribing authority (DEA number, pharmacy records)
- Institutional embeddedness (regulators dependent on the regulated)

Weave data directly into the physical reality. Numbers inside narrative paragraphs, not standalone callouts.

---

## 3. The Contradiction

State what was said, then juxtapose it with what the records show. Let contradictions accumulate naturally without numbering them. The weight of accumulated contradictions is more devastating than a tally.

---

## 4. Landing Line

Close on ONE of these:
- a quote
- a physical image
- a locked door
- a body fact
- a recorded line
- a documented irreversible act

Do NOT close with:
- abstract judgment
- editorial summary
- explanation of significance
- "and that is where/why/what..." formulas

**The last line should be concrete enough to photograph, specific enough to fact-check, or human enough to feel.**

---

## Section Transitions

Use thematic transitions that pull forward, not chronological ones:

**Good:**
- "That prescription changed everything."
- "The lockout didn't begin all at once. It was built in stages."
- "There is a pattern in Russell's records that doesn't prove every motive, but it does show a repeating mechanism."

**Bad:**
- "Then, on July 13..."
- "The next event occurred on..."
- "Subsequently..."

---

## Soft/Hard Framing (use sparingly -- conclusions only)

Offer the reader two interpretations:
1. The soft institutional version
2. The harder version the evidence actually supports

Let the reader choose. There's only one honest choice, and they know it.

Reserve this device for conclusions, reckonings, or major interpretive pivots. If overused, it reads like argument scaffolding rather than narrative discovery.
