# Good vs. Bad Examples

Side-by-side comparisons showing what to do and what not to do. Study these before writing.

---

## 1. Scenes vs. Timelines

### BAD (evidence catalog):
```
July 13. Coffee-ground vomiting documented.
July 17. Russell found on floor. EMS called for lift assist.
July 18. Weight recorded at 145 lbs, down from 160.
```

### GOOD (narrative):
```
What followed was not palliative gentleness. It was a rolling series of crises.
Russell fell repeatedly. He was found on the floor. He was found in vomit.
He vomited what was described as coffee-ground material, a classic sign of
gastrointestinal bleeding. Staff repeatedly called EMS for lift assists because
a single caregiver couldn't get him up. By early August he was described as
thin and frail.
```

**Why it works:** The reader discovers the decline as it unfolds. The evidence is all there -- the vomiting, the falls, the weight loss -- but woven into a paragraph that moves like a wave instead of a spreadsheet.

## 2. Contradictions: Numbered vs. Accumulated

### BAD (numbered cross-examination):
```
Lie one. Bartell said she didn't provide direct patient care.
The pharmacy records show otherwise.

Lie two. Bartell said Patricia's visits caused problems every time.
The facility logs document very few incidents tied to her presence.

Lie three. Bartell described Russell as improved.
He lost twenty-eight pounds in forty-three days.
```

### GOOD (accumulating contradictions):
```
Bartell said Patricia's visits caused problems essentially every time. That
doesn't match the facility's own records. Patricia visited dozens of times,
and the logs documented very few clear incidents tied specifically to her
presence. Many of Russell's worst episodes happened without her there.

Bartell also described Russell as improved -- eating, drinking, even gaining
a little weight, cheerful most of the time.

But the medical record documented 160 pounds on June 25 and 132 pounds on
August 7. That isn't weight gain. That is a collapse.
```

**Why it works:** The contradictions build on each other. The reader feels the lies accumulate. The weight numbers at the end are devastating because the reader just absorbed the claim of "improvement." No label needed. The juxtaposition IS the argument.

---

## 3. Conclusions: Recap vs. Thematic

### BAD (recapping known facts):
```
Start with the money. Nadine's Nest billed Russell's estate five thousand
five hundred dollars a month. The pharmacy records show over fourteen hundred
tablets in twenty-six days. The weight loss was twenty-eight pounds.
```

### GOOD (thematic opening):
```
It is possible to read this story as a local tragedy with bureaucratic rough
edges... The harder version is that Russell Bingaman entered a house where
the owner had economic control over his bed, practical control over the door...
```

**Why it works:** The reader already knows the numbers. Repeating them insults their attention. The thematic opening respects what the reader has absorbed and elevates it to its actual meaning.

---

## 4. Data Integration

### BAD (standalone callout):
```
DATA: 1,400+ tablets dispensed in 26 days
```

### GOOD (woven into narrative):
```
More than 1,400 tablets were dispensed in Russell's final twenty-six days.
He was a man who could barely swallow.
```

**Why it works:** The number hits because it's next to the physical reality. The reader feels the absurdity of 1,400 pills for a dying man who can't swallow. The data doesn't need a box around it. It needs a body next to it.

---

## 5. Emphasis: Empty vs. Earned

### BAD (empty emphasis):
```
The pharmacy records show fifty-five Risperidone tablets were dispensed
but never documented on the medication administration record.

That matters.
```

### GOOD (earned weight):
```
The pharmacy records show fifty-five Risperidone tablets were dispensed
but never documented on the medication administration record. Fifty-five
tablets entered the house and vanished from the paperwork.
```

**Why it works:** "That matters" is a crutch. It tells the reader to care instead of making them care. The restatement -- "entered the house and vanished from the paperwork" -- does the work that "that matters" pretended to do.

---

## 6. Hedging: Aggressive vs. Defensible

### BAD (overreaching):
```
Russell was placed on hospice within seven days of entering Nadine's Nest.
Bartell drugged him to death for profit.
```

### GOOD (hedged, then slammed shut):
```
It was not literally "on hospice in seven days." The cleaner and more
defensible timeline is worse in its own way: within a week, the decline
was already being documented as terminal; in less than two weeks, hospice
was back in place.

The harder question is whether the people around him responded to that
reality with careful care -- or with escalating sedation that made him
easier to manage, easier to classify as terminal, and easier to isolate
from the one person most likely to object.
```

**Why it works:** The hedge increases trust. The reader thinks "this writer is careful." Then the unhedged conclusion hits like a truck because the reader trusts the person delivering it.

---

## 7. Section Endings

### BAD (abstract verdict):
```
And that is the true cost of institutional failure -- a man reduced to
a billing code, his humanity erased by the very system designed to protect him.
```

### GOOD (concrete landing):
```
By the last week, Russell weighed 132 pounds. The staff called EMS
for a lift assist. A single caregiver couldn't get him off the floor.
```

**Why it works:** The abstract verdict sounds like a TED talk. The concrete landing puts the reader on the floor next to Russell. One generates admiration for the writer. The other generates rage at what happened. Only one of those serves the story.

---

## 8. Sensory Immersion: Visual Only vs. Mute Senses

### BAD (flat, visual-only):
```
The facility was a converted house. The living room had been turned into
a shared bedroom. Russell sat in a recliner by the window.
```

### GOOD (sound, smell, touch):
```
The facility smelled like institutional soap and something underneath it --
the sour, biological smell of a room where someone has been sick. The baby
monitor on the shelf cycled through static. Russell sat in a recliner, the
vinyl cracking under him when he shifted, his fingers working the armrest
fabric into pills.
```

**Why it works:** The reader is IN the room. They can smell it, hear the monitor, feel the cracking vinyl. Visual descriptions let the reader observe from a distance. Mute senses -- smell, sound, touch -- put them inside the scene. The distance disappears.

---

## 9. Status Details: Generic vs. Specific Character Artifacts

### BAD (generic adjectives):
```
Russell was an elderly farmer. He was a kind man who loved his family.
```

### GOOD (status details):
```
Russell wore his coat and hat even indoors. He ironed his own shirts until
he couldn't anymore. The wall behind his recliner had cattle pictures he'd
hung himself. When Patricia cut his hair, he asked what it cost.
```

**Why it works:** "Elderly farmer" is a category. Ironed shirts and cattle pictures and asking what a haircut costs -- that is a specific man. The reader grieves the specific man. Nobody grieves a category.

---

## 10. Data: Raw Numbers vs. Proportional Comparisons

### BAD (numbers without context):
```
Russell lost 28 pounds. He was prescribed 1,400 tablets. The facility
charged $5,500 per month.
```

### GOOD (proportional comparisons):
```
Russell lost twenty-eight pounds in forty-three days -- more than half a
pound every day, the kind of decline that would trigger an emergency
nutrition consult in any hospital. Staff dispensed more than 1,400 tablets
in his final twenty-six days, enough to fill a cereal bowl. The facility
charged $5,500 a month -- more than double what most Oregonians pay in rent.
```

**Why it works:** Numbers numb. Comparisons sting. "Half a pound a day" makes the reader do body math on themselves. "Enough to fill a cereal bowl" turns an abstract pill count into something they can see on their kitchen counter. "Double what most Oregonians pay in rent" puts the billing into the reader's own wallet.

---

## 11. Quotes: Bloated vs. On a Diet

### BAD (full bureaucratic transcript):
```
Bartell testified, "Well, I mean, we felt at the time that it was in the
best interest of the patient to, you know, make sure that the family visits
were managed in a way that was consistent with his care plan and didn't
cause any undue disruption to the facility environment."
```

### GOOD (paraphrase + surgical quote):
```
Bartell said Patricia's visits needed to be "managed" to avoid "disruption."
The facility's own logs documented almost none.
```

**Why it works:** The full quote lets the reader's eyes glaze through forty words of bureaucratic fog. The paraphrased version with two quoted words exposes the evasion in a single line. The reader hears the institutional language and then hits the factual contradiction like a wall.

---

## 12. Passive vs. Active Voice

### BAD (passive, actor hidden):
```
Russell was being given Chlorpromazine. The door was locked. His visits
were being restricted by the facility.
```

### GOOD (active, actor named):
```
Bartell prescribed Chlorpromazine for Russell. Staff locked the door.
The facility restricted Patricia's visits.
```

**Why it works:** Passive voice hides who did what. In investigative journalism, the actor IS the story. "Was being given" protects the prescriber. "Bartell prescribed" names her. Every passive construction is a small act of institutional protection. Strip it out.

---

## 13. Sentence Endings: Weak vs. Strong Final Word

### BAD (sentence dies at the end):
```
Russell lost twenty-eight pounds during the forty-three days he was there.
The pharmacy records were not included in the documentation that was provided.
```

### GOOD (power word at the end):
```
In forty-three days, Russell lost twenty-eight pounds.
The documentation they provided omitted the pharmacy records.
```

**Why it works:** The last word before the period is the one the reader carries into the next sentence. "There" and "provided" are dead weight. "Pounds" and "records" are loaded -- they carry the factual punch. Rearrange every sentence so the strongest word lands last.

---

## 14. The Pizza Slice: Wide Opening vs. Narrow Tip

### BAD (starting at the wide end):
```
Oregon's adult foster care system serves thousands of vulnerable residents
across the state. Oversight gaps have been documented for decades. The case
of Russell Bingaman illustrates the systemic failures that emerge when
licensing agencies lack enforcement authority.
```

### GOOD (starting at the narrow tip):
```
Patricia cut Russell's hair on a Tuesday. He sat in the kitchen chair and
held still, the way he always did. When she finished, he looked at her and
asked what it cost. She said nothing. He nodded. The old farmer instincts
were still in there, surfacing through the Alzheimer's like muscle memory.

Three weeks later, Bartell prescribed Chlorpromazine.
```

**Why it works:** The wide opening is a policy briefing. The narrow opening is a haircut. The reader who starts with the haircut will follow you into the Chlorpromazine. The reader who starts with "Oregon's adult foster care system" has already decided whether they care about guardianship reform, and most of them don't. Start with the man. The system reveals itself through what happened to him.
